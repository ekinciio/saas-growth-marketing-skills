# AI Citability Guide

This guide explains how AI search engines select and cite content, and how to optimize your content for maximum citation likelihood.

## What Is Citability?

Citability measures how likely an AI search engine is to select and quote a specific passage from your content when answering a user query. High-citability content is clear, factual, self-contained, and directly answers questions.

## The Optimal AI-Cited Passage

Research on AI search engine citation patterns reveals consistent characteristics of passages that get cited:

- **Length:** 134-167 words (the sweet spot for AI citations)
- **Structure:** Self-contained - makes sense without surrounding context
- **Content:** Fact-rich with specific data points, statistics, or definitions
- **Format:** Clear and direct, answering an implicit or explicit question
- **Tone:** Authoritative and neutral, avoiding hedging language

### Why 134-167 Words?

AI search engines need passages long enough to be informative but short enough to display as a citation. Passages in this range are:
- Long enough to provide complete, useful answers
- Short enough to fit within citation display limits
- Dense enough to contain meaningful information
- Concise enough to avoid filler content

## Citability Scoring Rubric (0-100)

Each content block is scored across four dimensions, each worth 25 points.

### 1. Structure Clarity (0-25 points)

Measures how well-organized and easy-to-parse the passage is for AI systems.

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 21-25 | Uses headers, bullet lists, or numbered lists; clear topic sentence; logical flow |
| 16-20 | Has a clear topic sentence; reasonable paragraph structure |
| 11-15 | Readable but could benefit from better formatting |
| 6-10 | Wall of text with no clear structure; hard to extract key points |
| 0-5 | Completely unstructured; rambling or disorganized |

**What earns high scores:**
- Descriptive headers (H2, H3) that signal content topic
- Bullet or numbered lists for multi-point information
- Definition format ("X is Y that does Z")
- Short paragraphs (3-5 sentences) with one clear idea each
- Bold or emphasized key terms

**What earns low scores:**
- Long paragraphs without breaks
- Missing or vague headers
- No formatting hierarchy
- Mixed topics within a single block

### 2. Factual Density (0-25 points)

Measures the concentration of specific, verifiable information.

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 21-25 | 3+ specific data points: numbers, dates, percentages, named entities |
| 16-20 | 2 specific data points with supporting context |
| 11-15 | 1 specific data point or several general claims |
| 6-10 | General claims without specific supporting data |
| 0-5 | Pure opinion or vague statements with no verifiable content |

**What earns high scores:**
- Statistics with sources ("According to a 2024 study, 73% of...")
- Specific dates, numbers, and measurements
- Named entities (people, organizations, products)
- Comparative data ("X is 3x faster than Y")
- Step counts or quantified processes

**What earns low scores:**
- Vague quantifiers ("many", "some", "a lot")
- Unsupported claims ("It is widely known that...")
- Filler phrases that add no information
- Repetition of the same point in different words

### 3. Self-Containment (0-25 points)

Measures whether the passage makes complete sense without surrounding context.

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 21-25 | Fully standalone; reader needs no prior context to understand |
| 16-20 | Mostly standalone; minor references to context that do not impede understanding |
| 11-15 | Requires some context but core message is clear |
| 6-10 | Heavily depends on surrounding content; uses pronouns without antecedents |
| 0-5 | Incomprehensible without reading the full page |

**What earns high scores:**
- Defines terms before using them
- Avoids dangling references ("this", "that", "the above")
- Includes enough context to stand as an independent answer
- States the subject explicitly rather than relying on page title

**What earns low scores:**
- Starts with "This is because..." or "As mentioned above..."
- Uses pronouns without clear antecedents ("They found that...")
- Assumes knowledge from previous sections
- References figures, tables, or sections by number

### 4. Question-Answer Format (0-25 points)

Measures how directly the passage answers a specific question.

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 21-25 | Directly and completely answers a clear, common question |
| 16-20 | Answers an implied question with a clear thesis statement |
| 11-15 | Contains useful information but does not directly address a question |
| 6-10 | Tangentially related to common queries; requires extraction |
| 0-5 | Does not answer any identifiable question |

**What earns high scores:**
- Starts with a direct answer ("The best way to X is Y")
- Follows a "What is X? X is..." pattern
- Provides how-to steps that answer a procedural question
- Includes comparison or decision-support information

**What earns low scores:**
- Promotional language without informational value
- Navigation or boilerplate text
- Introductory fluff before getting to the answer
- Content that raises questions without answering them

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

These patterns significantly reduce citability:

### 1. Vague Language
- **Bad:** "Our solution helps many companies improve their results significantly."
- **Good:** "The platform reduced customer churn by 23% across 150 enterprise accounts in Q3 2024."

### 2. Opinion Without Data
- **Bad:** "We believe this is the best approach for modern businesses."
- **Good:** "In a benchmark of 12 competing tools, this approach scored highest on accuracy (94.2%) and processing speed (1.3s average)."

### 3. Excessive Jargon
- **Bad:** "Leverage synergistic paradigm shifts to optimize cross-functional deliverables."
- **Good:** "Combine your marketing and sales data to find which campaigns drive the most revenue."

### 4. Pronoun-Heavy Openings
- **Bad:** "It helps them do this more efficiently than they could before."
- **Good:** "The automated reporting tool helps marketing teams generate weekly performance reports in under 5 minutes."

### 5. Fluffy Introductions
- **Bad:** "In today's fast-paced digital world, businesses need to stay ahead of the curve..."
- **Good:** "Email open rates dropped from 21.3% to 15.1% between 2022 and 2024, making subject line optimization critical."

### 6. Internal-Only References
- **Bad:** "See Section 3.2 for more details on implementation."
- **Good:** "Implementation requires three steps: install the SDK, configure API keys, and set up webhook endpoints."

## Optimization Checklist

Use this checklist when writing or revising content for AI citability:

- [ ] Each key passage is 134-167 words
- [ ] Every passage has at least 2 specific data points
- [ ] Passages make sense without reading the rest of the page
- [ ] Each passage answers a question someone might ask an AI
- [ ] Headers accurately describe the content that follows
- [ ] Lists are used for multi-point information
- [ ] Key terms are defined on first use
- [ ] No vague quantifiers ("many", "some", "various")
- [ ] No dangling references ("this", "that", "the above")
- [ ] No promotional fluff without supporting evidence
